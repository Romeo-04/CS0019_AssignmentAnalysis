Fidelity. Resolution. Scale.

How to Read the Diagram

Fidelity (How real?) – Do we model the true mechanics/sensors/policies?
Analogy: real aerodynamics vs. arcade flight.

Resolution (How detailed?) – How many distinct states or categories do we track?
Analogy: photo pixels or the number of customer types.

Scale (How big/long?) – Scope in space/time and population.
Analogy: map zoom (gate → terminal → nationwide) and time window (minutes → months).

The Venn shows which dial(s) we turned up for different kinds of airport models.

F only (High Fidelity, Low Resolution, Small Scale)

Example: e-Gate door/turnstile mechanism cycle model.

Why it lives here: It models the real mechanics of a single e-Gate, motor cycle timing, safety interlocks, sensor triggers, and failure probabilities, very accurately (high fidelity). It does not represent different passenger cohorts or multiple lanes, and it covers seconds per cycle rather than hours or terminals (low resolution, small scale).

What it answers: “What is the open/close cycle time and its variance?” “What availability can we expect (MTBF/MTTR) under continuous use?” “How long can the gate remain unlocked before a safety interlock engages?”

What it can’t answer: Queue length, required staffing, or airport-wide throughput, there’s no passenger flow here, just the device.

R only (High Resolution, low Fidelity, small Scale)

Example: Micro-queue sim of one counter: dozens of passenger states but service times are generic.

Why it lives here: Lots of categories (first-timer, elderly, mask/glasses, etc.), high resolution, but mechanics at each step are simplified (e.g., “service time = 40 seconds” with no sensor physics), so fidelity is modest and the scope is just one counter, small scale.

What it answers: “If I prioritize elderly or first-timers, what happens to fairness/wait?”

What it can’t answer: Device or sensor limits; airport-wide knock-on effects.

S only (High Scale, low Fidelity, low Resolution)

Example: Nationwide arrival forecast (passengers/day by airport; simple regression).

Why it lives here: Covers the whole country and long time horizons, high scale, but uses simple equations and few categories, low fidelity and low resolution.

What it answers: “How many arrivals per airport per day next month?” for high-level planning.

What it can’t answer: Minute-by-minute queues, bottlenecks, or sensor behavior.

F ∩ R (High Fidelity + High Resolution (limited Scale))

Example: Model of NAIA e-Gate arrivals (our working model).

Why it lives here: Models true steps (scan → biometrics → watchlist) with realistic failure/retry logic, high fidelity, and keeps meaningful cohorts (first-time users, repeat users, mask/glasses, referral reasons), high resolution. Scoped to one terminal / a few flight banks, scale is limited.

What it answers: “During tonight’s wide-body bank, how many e-Gates and marshals do we need to keep waits under 8 minutes?”

What it can’t answer: Multi-airport policies or month-long trends by itself.

F ∩ S (High Fidelity + High Scale (modest Resolution))

Example: NAIA airport grid-power model aggregated by terminals.

Why it lives here: Keeps real physics (power demand/thermal loads), high fidelity, and spans multiple terminals and long periods, high scale. Aggregates users/loads (few categories), modest resolution.

What it answers: “If Terminal 3 adds six e-Gate banks, what’s the peak load and backup power requirement this summer?”

What it can’t answer: Per-passenger queue behavior or detailed referral reasons.

R ∩ S (High Resolution + High Scale (modest Fidelity))

Example: Airport-wide simulation of all transfer passengers across all terminals.

Why it lives here: Tracks many passengers and paths (transfer security, immigration, people-movers) across all terminals and an entire day/week, high resolution and high scale. Checkpoint mechanics are black-boxed as service-time distributions, modest fidelity.

What it answers: “What’s the missed-connection rate by bank?” “How many transfer-security lanes to open from 17:30–19:00?”

What it can’t answer: Scanner physics or biometric thresholds.

F ∩ R ∩ S (All three high (the center))

Example: Full implementation of NAIA e-Gate arrivals for everyone & anytime.

Why it lives here: True-to-process (F), fine-grained per passenger (R), and airport-/multi-day scale (S).

What it promises: End-to-end “everything at once” answers.

Trade-off: Data-hungry, compute-heavy, hard to calibrate, rare in practice unless you have sustained data and engineering support.

Why is it important to know the differences?

Push Fidelity high → you trust mechanics but may lose breadth or detail.

Push Resolution high → you see fairness and cohort effects but must simplify physics or scope.

Push Scale high → you plan resources broadly but won’t capture second-by-second realities.
